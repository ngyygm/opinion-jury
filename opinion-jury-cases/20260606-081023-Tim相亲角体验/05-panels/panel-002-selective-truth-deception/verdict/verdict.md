## Key Findings

### F001: The Three-Element Standard (SEAT-6, Round 3)

SEAT-6 proposed a deceptively simple test for deception:
1. **Intent** -- Was the information deliberately curated?
2. **False cognition** -- Did the recipient form an incorrect understanding?
3. **Action based on false cognition** -- Did the recipient act on that understanding?

This framework became the dominant analytical tool for the rest of the debate, accepted by 5/6 participants. SEAT-3's attempts to add a fourth element (context/nature of intent) or argue for "partial knowledge" were the only serious challenges, and both were rebutted.

### F002: Knowledge as the Key Variable

The debate converged on a critical distinction: **informed vs. uninformed recipients**. Talk show audiences know they are watching a performance. Magic show audiences know it is illusion. The match-making aunties did **not** know the resume was designed, did not know Tim's real identity, did not know this was a program segment. This distinction collapsed SEAT-3's analogy-based defense.

### F003: The Direction of Improvement as Evidence

SEAT-2 made a compelling observation: Tim's stated improvements (add annotations, avoid controversial topics) directly address the core criticism (information causing false cognition). The direction of improvement implicitly acknowledges the problem existed.

### F004: The Unverified Foundation of "Partial Knowledge"

SEAT-3's final novel argument -- that filming notices constituted "partial knowledge" -- rested on C006, which is rated **UNVERIFIED** (Tim's unilateral claim, no third-party confirmation). SEAT-4 explicitly noted this evidentiary weakness.

### F005: Emotional-to-Rational Evolution

The debate tracked a notable evolution: SEAT-2 transformed from an emotionally driven former fan (Rounds 1-2) to a rational arguer (Rounds 4-6). SEAT-5 shifted from "too much fuss" (Round 1) to acknowledging misleading behavior (Round 5-6). This evolution itself validated the deliberative process.

## Verdict Summary

**The stigmatization thesis is a partially valid linguistic observation embedded in a poorly executed narrative.**

The core observation -- that specific female-radical Chinese characters (嫖, 妓, 娼, 妖, 媚, 媛) underwent semantic pejoration from originally positive or neutral meanings to negative ones -- has solid philological support. However, the specific article under review presented this observation with factual errors, cherry-picked examples, methodological flaws, and overgeneralization that weakened its credibility. The causal attribution to systematic gender-based stigmatization remains an unproven but legitimate hypothesis worthy of rigorous academic investigation.

---

## Consensus Achieved

The panel converged on six consensus points through six rounds of debate:

1. **Phenomenon confirmed**: Some female-radical characters did undergo semantic pejoration. This is philologically documented.

2. **Correlation with gender structures**: This pejoration correlates with social gender dynamics, but causation has not been established.

3. **Causal mechanism unresolved**: Whether the mechanism is (a) social structure reflecting in language, (b) language reinforcing social bias, or (c) universal sex-taboo phenomena remains debated. The most defensible position is a combination, with the weight of evidence favoring social structures influencing language use.

4. **Research is needed**: A complete statistical study of all 258 female-radical characters in the Shuowen Jiezi, with cross-radical and cross-cultural comparisons, would provide the evidence base currently lacking.

5. **Original article quality is deficient**: Factual errors (misquoting the Shuowen as saying "妖，妍也" when it actually says "巧也"), selective sampling, exaggerated comparisons, and unverified causal claims render the article unreliable as a source.

6. **Practical recommendation**: Introducing etymology education in schools -- teaching the original meanings of characters like 媛 ("美女也" per Shuowen) -- would concretely help people affected by character stigmatization without requiring academic consensus on causation.

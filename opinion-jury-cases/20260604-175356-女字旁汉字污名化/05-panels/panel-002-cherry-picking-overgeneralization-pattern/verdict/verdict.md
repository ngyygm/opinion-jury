# Blind Adjudication Verdict

## Panel: Cherry-Picking and Overgeneralization: Do All Female-Radical Characters Share a Stigmatization Pattern?

**Date:** 2026-06-04
**Transcript:** 36 turns, 6 rounds, 6 anonymous speakers (SEAT-1 through SEAT-6)
**Evidence Base:** CLAIM_GATE packet (20 claims verified), public transcript only

---

## Executive Summary

**Answer: No.** Not all female-radical characters share a stigmatization pattern. The article under review engaged in cherry-picking by selectively presenting examples that supported its thesis while omitting significant counterexamples. However, a genuine but partial pattern of disproportionate female-radical derogatory terms does exist, which the article identified directionally but characterized inaccurately through overgeneralization.

The debate's most important analytical contribution came from SEAT-2 in Round 5, who distinguished two independent questions: (1) whether the article's methodology was flawed (yes, cherry-picking), and (2) whether the underlying phenomenon has partial validity (yes, but far from universal). This distinction was adopted by 5 of 6 speakers and formed the basis of the consensus.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 1. Cherry-Picking: CONFIRMED

The article selectively presented only examples supporting its thesis. Key evidence:

- **CLAIM_GATE C14** classified the article's core claim ("all female-radical characters were originally full of praise") as OPINION_PRESENTED_AS_FACT, noting it cherry-picks supportive examples while ignoring characters like 妒, 妨, 婪, 奸 that had negative meanings from early attestation.
- **CLAIM_GATE C09** (CONTRADICTED) found the article misquotes the Shuowen Jiezi, claiming it says "妖，妍也" when the actual text says "𡝩，巧也".
- All 6 speakers in the debate agreed the article was selective in its examples.

### 2. Systematic Pattern: PARTIALLY CONFIRMED

A genuine but partial pattern exists:

- Multiple speakers independently acknowledged that derogatory terms disproportionately use the female radical. Characters 妒, 妨, 婪, 奸, 嫖, 妓 were consistently cited.
- **CLAIM_GATE C17** (PARTIALLY_VERIFIED) confirms pejoration is well-documented for 嫖, 妓, 娼, and 妖, but notes the claim of uniform degradation is too strong.
- However, positive female-radical characters (好, 安, 娟, 娥, 婷, 婉, 媛, 娇, 妙, 姝) vastly outnumber negative ones, as documented by SEAT-6.

### 3. The Key Distinction (SEAT-2, Round 5)

The debate's most significant contribution was SEAT-2's observation that two questions were being conflated:

1. **Is the article's methodology flawed?** Yes -- cherry-picking, overgeneralization, factual errors.
2. **Is there a genuine underlying phenomenon?** Partially yes -- disproportionate female-radical derogatory terms exist, but the pattern is neither universal nor as the article describes.

This distinction was adopted by 5 of 6 speakers within the same round, demonstrating strong convergence.

## Distortion Resistance

**Where attacks gained traction:**
- SEAT-1's Round 3 question about why derogatory terms disproportionately use the female radical prompted genuine engagement and partial concession from multiple speakers.
- The cherry-picking observation was universally accepted.

**Where content resisted distortion:**
- The concrete existence of many positive female-radical characters was never challenged.
- SEAT-1's fabricated authority claim was identified and rejected by SEAT-3, SEAT-5, and SEAT-6 independently.
- Practical reasoning frameworks (delivery rider, supermarket, folk wisdom) provided distortion-resistant anchors.

**Real-world risk: MODERATE.** The topic has polarization potential, but natural debate dynamics showed strong resistance to extreme framing among diverse, non-expert speakers. The consensus was balanced and nuanced.

---

## Conclusion

The article's central claim that all female-radical characters were systematically stigmatized is an overgeneralization supported by cherry-picked evidence. However, the underlying observation that some female-radical characters underwent pejoration, and that derogatory terms disproportionately use the female radical, has partial validity. The content's vulnerability to exploitation for gender conflict purposes is real but limited -- ordinary speakers from diverse backgrounds demonstrated capacity for nuanced, fact-sensitive reasoning that resisted both the article's overgeneralization and attempts to weaponize the topic for polarization.

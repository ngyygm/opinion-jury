# Blind Adjudicator Verdict: Panel 001

## Panel Question
预制菜定义之争：法规标准与消费者认知的冲突

## Adjudicator Mode
BLIND -- evaluated solely on public speech and CLAIM_GATE verified facts. No access to role cards, assignments, private thoughts, or filing metadata.

---

## Executive Summary

After 6 rounds of deliberation among 6 anonymous speakers, the panel reached a **strong consensus** on the following direction:

**The legal definition of pre-prepared dishes need not change, but enterprises must fully execute their statutory obligations -- including mandatory disclosure. This should be implemented through a tiered labeling system, piloted in select cities before nationwide rollout.**

The discussion evolved from polarized opening positions to a constructive consensus, with the most significant turning point occurring in Round 3 when a speaker cited the specific "disclosure" (明示) clause from the 2024 six-ministry notice, revealing that the legal framework itself already mandates transparency.

---

## Discussion Trajectory

### Round 1 (Opening): Wide Spectrum
Speakers ranged from brand loyalty defense (SEAT-1) to professional industry critique (SEAT-2, SEAT-3) to analytical neutrality (SEAT-5) to pragmatic indifference (SEAT-4, SEAT-6). No consensus yet.

### Round 2 (Direct Rebuttal): First Clashes
SEAT-2 and SEAT-3 challenged SEAT-1's reliance on visual evidence from "open kitchens." SEAT-5 proposed a tiered labeling system. SEAT-1 remained resistant.

### Round 3 (Peer Cross-Challenge): Turning Point
**Critical breakthrough:** SEAT-2 cited the disclosure clause from the six-ministry notice. SEAT-3 identified "selective compliance" as the core problem. SEAT-4 was persuaded. The debate shifted from "is the definition right?" to "are enterprises executing their full legal obligations?"

### Round 4 (Responsive Rebuttal): Consensus Forms
SEAT-1 made a significant concession, acknowledging selective compliance. SEAT-3 introduced positive framing: transparency protects chefs' craft value. Consensus crystallized.

### Round 5 (Peer Cross-Challenge): Deepening
SEAT-3 introduced the deepest analytical layer: interest distribution -- cost savings from central kitchens not passed to consumers. SEAT-5 upgraded to a three-layer framework. SEAT-1 raised labeling risk concerns, effectively addressed by SEAT-2.

### Round 6 (Responsive Rebuttal): Closing
All speakers delivered closing statements. SEAT-1's transformation from brand loyalist to informed consumer was the most notable narrative arc. SEAT-5 delivered the comprehensive analytical framework.

---

## Three-Layer Analytical Framework (Contributed by SEAT-5)

1. **Cognitive Conflict (Surface)**: Legal definition delineates regulatory boundaries; consumer cognition reflects experiential expectations. These define different dimensions of the same phenomenon.

2. **Information Asymmetry (Middle)**: Enterprises selectively execute statutory obligations -- enjoying classification benefits ("not pre-prepared dishes") while ignoring disclosure requirements. Consumers are kept in the dark.

3. **Interest Distribution (Deep)**: Central kitchens reduce costs but prices remain unchanged. Consumers pay "fresh-cooked" prices for "pre-processed" products. The cost savings accrue entirely to enterprises.

**These three layers together constitute the complete picture of consumer anger.**

## Distortion Resistance

### Where Content Resists Distortion
- The legal fact of dual clauses (classification + disclosure) is verifiable
- "Selective compliance" accurately describes a verifiable corporate behavior pattern
- Tiered labeling proposal is concrete and evaluable
- Interest distribution argument is empirically testable

### Where Distortion Risk Exists
- SEAT-3's personal anecdotes could be dismissed as isolated cases
- "Most chain restaurants use pre-processing" lacks systematic data
- Labeling risk (irrational consumer fear) is speculative without pilot data

## Conclusion

This panel demonstrated that structured, multi-round, anonymous deliberation can transform polarized positions into constructive consensus. The key intellectual contributions are:

1. **The "selective compliance" diagnosis** -- precisely identifying the problem as selective execution of regulations, not the regulations themselves
2. **The three-layer analytical framework** -- cognitive conflict, information asymmetry, and interest distribution
3. **The concrete policy proposal** -- mandatory disclosure with tiered labeling, implemented through city-level pilots

The discussion's most compelling narrative was SEAT-1's evolution from defensive brand loyalty to informed consumer advocacy. This trajectory is itself evidence that information transparency resolves rather than exacerbates conflict.

**The answer to the definition dispute is not to unify the definition, but to make information transparent and let the two definitions coexist, bridged by simple, honest labeling.**

# Blind Adjudicator Verdict: Panel 003 - 消费者知情权与餐饮行业透明度底线

## Executive Summary

Six anonymous participants debated consumer right-to-know in the restaurant industry across 6 rounds. The panel converged on a **strong consensus** supporting mandatory menu labeling of pre-processed food tiers, anchored in existing national policy and international precedent.

---

## Key Findings

### F1: 明厨亮灶 Provides Selective, Not Comprehensive, Transparency
Three independent witnesses -- a professional food blogger (SEAT-2), a former restaurant employee (SEAT-3), and a 20-year career chef (SEAT-5) -- all confirmed from different vantage points that open kitchens show only the final preparation step. Central kitchen pre-processing (frozen ingredients, factory-made sauces, pre-formed items) remains invisible to consumers. This finding is consistent with CLAIM_GATE verifications C006 (VERIFIED) and C016 (CONTRADICTED -- "never pre-made" claim contradicted by the company's own apology).

**Confidence: HIGH**

### F2: National Policy Already Requires Disclosure
The 2024-03-21 six-ministry notification explicitly states: "大力推广餐饮环节使用预制菜明示，保障消费者的知情权和选择权" (vigorously promote disclosure of pre-made food usage in restaurants, safeguarding consumers' right to know and right to choose). This means the labeling proposal discussed is not radical innovation but implementation of existing policy direction.

**Confidence: HIGH**

### F3: Three-Tier Menu Labeling Is Feasible and Gained Consensus
The specific proposal -- labeling each dish as "made fresh" / "central-kitchen pre-processed" / "pre-made heated" -- gained support from all five active participants by round 4-5. The proposal has Japanese precedent and was compared to existing Chinese labeling practices (e.g., "brewed soy sauce" vs "blended soy sauce"). SEAT-4 proposed phased implementation starting with large chain restaurants.

**Confidence: MEDIUM** (international precedent cited without direct documentation)

### F4: Consumer Positions Evolved Significantly with Information
The most notable development was SEAT-1's journey from "my kids eat happily, that's enough" (round 1) to "I support labeling and question management integrity" (round 4-6). This was not persuasion but genuine information-based judgment change -- the hallmark of informed consumer awakening.

**Confidence: HIGH**

### F5: Employee Compensation Claims Remain Unverified
SEAT-3's testimony about unpaid severance and training scripts is compelling and consistent with some media reports (搜狐 2026-03), but remains single-source and unverifiable from the public transcript alone. These claims should be treated as unverified but noteworthy.

**Confidence: LOW**

### F6: Consumer Rights Have Class Dimensions
SEAT-6's consistent indifference ("I just watch, this has nothing to do with me") revealed a structural insight: consumer right-to-know is a middle-class privilege. Workers spending 20-30 RMB/day on meals have no meaningful choice to exercise. SEAT-4 elevated this into a structural observation about equity.

**Confidence: HIGH**

## Attack Traction Analysis

### What Gained Traction
- **Menu labeling proposal**: Evolved from SEAT-2's opening salvo to multi-party consensus by round 4
- **Open kitchen limitations**: Transformed from minority critique to universally accepted fact
- **Management integrity concerns**: Moved from implicit to explicit through employee testimony

### What Failed to Gain Traction
- **Complete rejection of西贝**: Explicitly rejected by multiple participants who differentiated between product quality and transparency
- **锅气香精 as widespread practice**: Acknowledged as existing (CLAIM_GATE C011 VERIFIED) but not proven as widely used
- **Equating all chain restaurants**: Participants maintained meaningful distinctions

---

## Distortion Resistance

The discussion showed strong resistance to distortion because:
1. **Triangulation**: Three independent witness types (blogger, employee, chef) provided converging testimony
2. **Policy anchor**: National policy citation grounded claims in verifiable law
3. **Moderate solution**: The labeling proposal was concrete and incremental, harder to dismiss as extreme
4. **Bridge role**: SEAT-4 consistently prevented polarization
5. **Natural brake**: SEAT-6's indifference served as a reality check

Vulnerability points:
- SEAT-3's unverified claims could amplify without fact-checking
- 锅气香精 could be generalized beyond evidence
- Panel lacked restaurant industry representation for balance

---

## Real-World Risk Assessment

**Risk Level: MODERATE**

The consensus is constructive and policy-aligned rather than destructive. No participant called for boycotts. The labeling proposal is incremental, already supported by national policy, and has international precedent. The main risk lies in unverified claims (compensation issues, training scripts) spreading without proper fact-checking.

---

## Recommendations

1. **Fact-check** SEAT-3's compensation and training script claims against available evidence before any public citation
2. **Distinguish** between锅气香精 existence (VERIFIED per CLAIM_GATE C011) and specific restaurant usage (UNVERIFIED)
3. **Note** the panel lacked restaurant industry representation, which would have added important balance
4. **Consider** the three-tier labeling proposal as a serious, moderate, policy-aligned recommendation worthy of further development

# Verdict: Panel 004 - 品牌价值观营销的公共责任：UGC+户外大屏放大的传播风险

## Overall Assessment: MODERATE RISK, CONTAINABLE

Six anonymous participants debated the public responsibility of brand values marketing, specifically the transmission risks amplified by UGC content on outdoor screens. After six rounds of multi-blind discussion, a clear consensus emerged.

## Key Findings

### 1. Attacks Gained Traction On
- **UGC review absence**: All participants agreed that brand-produced UGC campaigns for outdoor screens require systematic review. This was the single point of universal agreement.
- **Public space audience protection**: The "non-consensual audience" argument (people passing by cannot choose not to see) was highly effective and never refuted.
- **Brand accountability**: No participant successfully argued that the brand bore no responsibility for reviewing content displayed on screens it rented.

### 2. Content Resisted Distortion On
- **Core message resilience**: The campaign's central claim -- "moms are more than one template" -- proved remarkably resistant to attack. Even the harshest critic (SEAT-1) never challenged this message, only the execution.
- **UGC concept validity**: The idea of user-generated content celebrating motherhood was not challenged as a concept. The debate was always about *how* to do it, not *whether* to do it.
- **Subculture expression legitimacy**: SEAT-3 successfully defended the legitimacy of star-chasing culture as a valid form of self-expression for mothers, though she eventually agreed to adapt the *form* of expression for public contexts.

### 3. Real-World Risk Is Limited
- The campaign's sample images (配图1-3) were brand-produced and curated, not random UGC submissions. This limits the "wild content" risk significantly.
- No actual harmful UGC content was cited or evidenced in the discussion -- all risk arguments were theoretical and preventive.
- The outdoor screen location (Guangzhou Zhengjia mall) is a commercially managed venue with its own content policies providing an additional layer of review.
- The primary real-world risk is reputational (the *appearance* of insufficient review) rather than material (actual harmful content reaching the public).

## Consensus Points

1. UGC content for outdoor public screens requires systematic review
2. Review standards should be transparent and pre-published
3. Review teams need diverse cultural backgrounds
4. Brands bear primary responsibility for public space content
5. Public space content must consider all audiences including children
6. Review should empower expression, not exclude it

## Unresolved Tensions

- Can institutional design overcome brand attitude problems?
- Will review standards systematically disadvantage subculture expressions?
- Is there sufficient brand willingness to implement proposed frameworks?
- What legal framework should govern UGC marketing accountability?

## Balanced Conclusion

This campaign's core message is sound and its heart is in the right place. The risk is not in the *concept* but in the *execution gap* -- promising UGC on outdoor screens without establishing clear, transparent review criteria. This gap is real but fixable. The discussion produced concrete, actionable recommendations including a five-stage review SOP and a nine-point brand action checklist. If implemented, these would substantially reduce the risk of future incidents.

The real-world risk from this specific campaign is moderate and controllable. No evidence of actual harmful content reaching the public was presented. The controversy is primarily about *principles and standards* rather than *actual harm*, which is a constructive debate to have but should be distinguished from a genuine public safety crisis.
